What isn't the Open Web?

Apple owns iOS and the underlying APIs & frameworks - This translates into a captive App Store audience and taking a 30% cut. - Apple also restricts what you can buy and install based on where you are in the world - If you're outside the US, you might be out of luck. - That’s not to say you get nothing. The App Store is an undeniably effective machine for marketing and distributing iOS apps. - But, it's also the only way to market & distribute iOS apps. - And iOS apps only work on Apple devices.

What isn't the Open Web?

Google's Android is nifty, and it's published as open source. That's not bad. Like Apple, Google takes a 30% cut of apps sold through the Play Store - And, again, you get marketing and distribution help. Android works on many vendors' devices - you can even install an alternative App Store from Amazon. That's all miles better than Apple, IMO. But, and I'm really sorry if this sounds like FUD, there are catches that worry me: - For example, Android software runs on Dalvik. - Dalvik is a reimplementation of Java. - They call it a "cleanroom" reimplementation, but that's in dispute. - Java is not entirely a free and open industry standard. - Java has complex licensing. Some of it is under the GPL. Some of it is not. Sun Microsystems wanted to encourage Java's spread, yet still have ways to tap in and make money. - These days, Oracle owns Java. They disagree that Google should be free to employ a clone of Java without paying a great deal of money for it. - http://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2010/08/oracles-java-lawsuit-undermines-its-open-source-credibility/ - http://www.itworld.com/mobile-wireless/278559/java-creator-unhappy-oracle-trial-outcome Apple & Google & Oracle are big companies, so they can fight this out. - But, personally, I hate feeling like my career & industry is underfoot for those fights.

What is the Open Web?

No one owns the Open Web - Something to notice here is that there are no pictures of vendors or company logos here. Just technologies. - HTTP, HTML, CSS, and JavaScript were all created as open industry standards. - Anyone is free to create their own implementations of web technologies.

25 Years of Experience

- You know, the web is 25 years old in 2014! http://www.webat25.org/ - The Mozilla Project turned 16 this year, and the Mozilla Foundation will be 11 in July - So, that's a long history of technology and source code out in the open. - Now, of course, I'd be dumb not to admit that some of those 25 years were full of hacks, blind alleys, and total blunders. But, we've learned quite a lot along the way about how to build software for a distributed world-wide-web. - But we've had 25 years to build frameworks and address scaling and all those mistakes along the way have produced wisdom that's now embedded in our code. - In fact, at this conference, you'll find a lot of folks talking about the latest & greatest of this stuff. So, I won't waste too much time enumerating the state of the art.

Apps & the Open Web

Distributed Code on Demand

- Remember back when the iPhone first came out? Steve Jobs said iPhone apps were web apps - These things are as valid now as they were back in 2007. (There's more to say about this slide, but I'll get to that in a few minutes.) - But, on the Open Web, you don't need a gatekeeper. Just come to my site and you're running my code, from anywhere in the world. - Most iOS and Android apps require a full redownload to install a new version. - Some larger games only ship a patcher to the store and do their own resource management internally - I mean, I guess that's better than buying CDs at the mall and using a Palm HotSync cradle to install apps. - On the web, you don't need to download the whole thing, just fetch the code updates you need. You can do this with individual resources, or bundle things up. - Remember emerging markets and low bandwidth? - The web has mechanisms for caching and offline access - That's like the #1 question I hear.

The Web Wasn't Ready

“Telling developers that web apps are iPhone apps just doesn’t fly. Think about it this way: If web apps — which are only accessible over a network;
which don’t get app icons in the iPhone home screen;
which don’t have any local data storage
— are such a great way to write software for iPhone, then why isn’t Apple using this technique for any of their own iPhone apps?”
— John Gruber (11 Jun 2007)
Now, there's an elephant in the room. The web wasn't ready for apps. Everything John Gruber says here was true about the web of 2007. But, before the iPhone, there was precedent for web apps. In 2005, Apple introduced the Dashboard for OS, full of little web-based widgets that in retrospect look suspiciously like apps. And with regard to the web, Apple introduced the Canvas element for WebKit back in 2004. That let us do things like draw clock hands in Dashboard widgets - that was mindblowing back then. Other browser vendors like Mozilla followed their lead and added Canvas support too. So, when the iPhone arrived (and I was still poking around on my PalmOS phone) I thought we were headed for an interesting world where Dashboard widgets - and coincidentally the web - would get levelled up. But then, in less than a year, Apple departed from the web strategy and released a Cocoa-based SDK. And as a web-head I was sad. I don't have any inside dirt at Apple, but I feel like someone lost an argument. But it's understandable: Levelling up the web is a lot of work that doesn't promise a profit along the way. In contrast, it's obvious that Cocoa Touch and the App Store has been wildly successful for Apple. But, Mozilla and other Open Web friends (some actually working for Apple) took these failings as challenges. Today, none of John Gruber's criticisms remain true. And, in fact, Firefox OS is the ultimate response to the final point: Every app in Firefox OS is built using web technology. But, there's still a lot of work left to do.
